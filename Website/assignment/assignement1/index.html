My Web perspective response

Saul Enriquez

The author of the article is delusional about online communities being destroyed for malicious reasons. She is suggesting that the platforms were suppressed when in reality, the platforms experienced natural user changes over time. Instead of acknowledging that social media landscapes shift due to evolving trends and audience preferences, she tries to create a conspiracy. " But the long and short of it is: Russia killed Livejournal. The Russian government, using corporate entities.” This statement wrongly suggests that an external force killed LiveJournal when, in reality, users simply moved on to other platforms that better suited their needs like facebook and twitter. The internet is always changing and no platform is entitled to relevance.

Another exaggerated claim she makes is that online communities are being weaponized against each other, as if users are being forced into conflict rather than simply engaging in discussions. “These were our spaces, little people who just wanted to connect. And one by one, they get turned into battlefields where we have to fight just as hard to exist as we do in the real world”. This statement exaggerates the natural progression of online spaces. The internet has never been free of conflict, and platforms evolve based on how the world interacts with them. Instead of complaining about the presence of trolls or misinformation, we should take responsibility for curating our experiences and adapting to the environments.

The author also CONTRADICTS herself by admitting that content comes from users rather than the platform itself, yet she still blames corporations for communities fading away. “People dont really want what little a site thats now solely supported by the free content created by the community has to offer. They want the community.” If the value of a platform is in the people rather than the framework, then why does it matter if a platform declines? People can move on to new digital spaces, which is exactly what has happened repeatedly throughout internet history. The idea that these communities were taken away ignores the reality that users actively choose to migrate elsewhere.

The authors argument about Elon Musk and Twitter is another example of misplaced blame. She portrays Musk as singlehandedly responsible for the platforms decline, failing to recognize that businesses naturally change direction based on market forces. “One unimaginably rich man should not be able to take away the livelihoods of millions of people just paying the fucking rent.” This is a stupid take that ignores the fact that no single person can destroy an entire digital economy. Companies are profit driven and social media platforms will always prioritize sustainability. If artists and creators relied solely on Twitter for income, that was always a risky decision, as no platform has historically lasted forever.

She also acts as though platforms must be the sole enforcers of moderation rather than empowering users to share their own experiences. “Use good moderation to make non-monster humans feel safe expressing themselves and feel nice about site so they use it more.” Platforms already offer tools to mute, block, and filter content. Instead of expecting a social media site to micromanage every interaction, people should take advantage of these features. The authors frustration seems less about actual suppression and more about the unwillingness to take PERSONAL RESPONSIBILITY for navigating online spaces.

Despite the author being born in 1979 she does not seem to understand digital communities. The author also has unreBalistic expectations for others to conform to her standards of morality. The internet is always evolving and multiple times throughout the article she contradicts herself. The internet is not being stolen or erased it is simply EVOLVING.
